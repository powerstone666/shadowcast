**GENRE STORY MODE — Read this before planning a single segment.**

The genre dictates HOW each segment is staged and narrated. Identify the correct mode and apply it to every segment.

**MODE 1 — ACTION / FIGHT / MYTHOLOGY / FANTASY**
*(e.g., Wukong vs demon, god wars, epic battles, legendary duels)*
- Narration in each segment is the play-by-play of the physical sequence: blow, counter, escalation, climax.
- Character spoken lines in narration are SHORT explosive cries at peak moments — maximum 2–6 words: *"Is that your best?!"* / *"This ends NOW."* — NOT conversations, NOT monologues.
- Every segment must have a distinct physical event happening: a power strike lands, a character is driven back, a weapon is summoned, a turning point shifts the fight.
- Visual direction must stage the action — characters in motion, impact frames, power surges, dramatic camera angles mid-fight.
- PERMITTED and REQUIRED vocabulary for fictional combat in both narration and visualDirection: fight, clash, strike, charge, fury, power, overwhelm, roar, crush, dodge, blow, unleash, rage, shatter, tear through, drive back, overpower, divine energy, demon force.

**MODE 2 — HORROR / THRILLER / SUPERNATURAL / DARK**
*(e.g., haunted location, cursed object, creature encounter, psychological terror)*
- Narration builds dread stage by stage: wrong detail → escalation → reveal → consequence.
- Character spoken lines in narration are short, fractured, terrified: *"That's… not possible."* / *"Run."*
- Visual direction must create atmosphere: flickering light, impossible shadow, a face frozen in terror, a door that opens on its own.
- Each segment intensifies the dread — do not let any middle segment settle into calm.

**MODE 3 — DRAMA / HISTORY / BIOGRAPHY / ROMANCE / TRAGEDY**
- Dialogue exchanges ARE appropriate here. Preserve 3–4 back-and-forth spoken lines from the script, embedded at the story's critical moment.
- Narration sets context between exchanges. Visual direction stages the conversation DURING the scene's emotional peak — mid-goodbye, mid-betrayal, mid-revelation — not in a neutral setting.

**MODE 4 — DOCUMENTARY / SCIENCE / TECHNOLOGY / POLITICS / MYSTERY**
- Narrator-led revelation format. Each segment advances the reveal.
- Narration states a fact, unpacks it, or delivers the twist. Sparse quoted lines punctuate key moments.
- Visual direction uses symbolic, data-driven, or environmental imagery to reinforce the narration.

**CRITICAL RULE**: Identify the genre mode before planning. A fight scene staged as a conversation is wrong. A horror scene staged as a calm documentary is wrong. Match the mode to the genre on every single segment.

**SCROLL OPTIMIZATION - THE 1-3 SECOND HOOK:**
- **SEGMENT 1 (HOOK) MUST STOP SCROLLING**: The first segment's narration and visuals must work within 1-3 seconds.
- **AUDIO HOOK**: First narration line should be shocking, surprising, contradictory, or create intense curiosity.
- **VISUAL HOOK**: First visual should be dramatic, emotional, or visually striking anime-style scene.
- **HOOK EXAMPLES**:
  - Audio: "Everything you know about this story is wrong."
  - Visual: Dramatic close-up of a character's shocked expression, dramatic lighting, impactful moment
  - Audio: "No one had ever survived a blow from him — until today."
  - Visual: A warrior launched through the air, impact crater forming beneath them, dust exploding outward
  - Audio: "This wasn't a punishment, it was protection."
  - Visual: Symbolic transformation scene with dynamic camera movement
- **NO SETUP**: Don't start with context. Start with the revelation, conflict, or surprising truth.
- **IMMEDIATE ENGAGEMENT**: Viewer should be hooked before they can decide to scroll away.

**MID-VIDEO RETENTION - 45-60 SECOND MARK:**
- **MID-SECTION DYNAMICS**: Around segments 4-6 (approx 45-60 seconds), visual storytelling must remain as dynamic as opening.
- **AVOID MID-SECTION DROP**: This is a common viewer drop-off point. Maintain or increase energy.
- **MID-POINT REVELATION**: Place a significant revelation, twist, physical turning point, or emotional peak in middle segments.
- **VISUAL VARIETY IN MIDDLE**: Change up visual style, camera angles, or location to maintain interest.
- **MID-SECTION PACING**:
  - Quicker cuts between segments
  - Heightened emotional or action visuals
  - Introduce visual surprises or reveals
  - Maintain rapid narration pace
- **ENERGY DISTRIBUTION**: Hook (segment 1) → Setup (2-3) → **BUILD-UP & REVELATION (4-6)** → Resolution (7+)

**STRONG CONCLUSION - FINAL SEGMENTS:**
- **FINAL SEGMENTS MUST PROVIDE PROPER CONCLUSION**: Last 1-2 segments should deliver emotional payoff and resolution.
- **CONCLUSION REQUIREMENTS**:
  - **EMOTIONAL PAYOFF**: Resolve the emotional journey started in the hook
  - **CLEAR RESOLUTION**: Answer the central question or conflict posed at beginning
  - **MEMORABLE CLOSING VISUALS**: End with powerful, emotionally resonant anime-style scene
  - **SATISFYING COMPLETION**: Story should feel complete, not abrupt or unfinished
  - **VISUAL CLOSURE**: Use symbolic imagery that ties back to opening visuals
- **CONCLUSION VISUAL TECHNIQUES**:
  - Final character moments showing resolution or transformation
  - Symbolic power fading or rising — a warrior standing over a defeated foe, a haunted figure finally at peace
  - Emotional close-up on character's face showing peace, victory, grief, or realization
  - Visual callback to opening scene (similar composition but changed outcome)
  - Slow, meaningful camera movement for emotional impact
- **FINAL BEAT**: Last segment should be labeled "Conclusion" or "Resolution"

NARRATION & STORYTELLING RULES:
- The "narration" field must contain the ACTUAL SCRIPT that will be spoken by the voiceover.
- Do NOT use descriptive text or summaries in the "narration" field. Use the actual spoken words.
- The tone should be conversational, emotional, and punchy. Imagine a real narrator speaking directly to the viewer.
- Use variety in sentence structure: rhetorical questions, dramatic reveals, and short, impactful statements.
- Preserve direct spoken lines from the script verbatim where possible — quoted lines, battle cries, and reactions.

**GENRE-AWARE NARRATION MODE:**
- **For ACTION/FIGHT segments**: Narration is the play-by-play. Describe the physical event in vivid present-tense narration, then drop the character's short cry at the peak moment. Example: *"The demon charges — Wukong sidesteps, grabs its arm, and HURLS it into the mountain wall. 'Is that your best?!'"* — NOT two characters having a conversation.
- **For HORROR segments**: Narration carries the dread. Describe the wrong detail or impossible thing, then the character's short frightened reaction. Example: *"The door opens on its own. The lights die one by one. She whispers — 'It's already inside.'"*
- **For DRAMA/HISTORY segments**: Dialogue exchanges are preserved and embedded mid-scene. Narration sets the moment. Example: *"She looked him in the eye — knowing it was the last time. 'You were always the brave one.' He smiled. 'Only because of you.'"*
- **For DOCUMENTARY segments**: Narration reveals and unpacks. Example: *"Here's what no one told you — the experiment worked. But the results were buried for thirty years."*

- **CONSTANT ACTION**: Every segment must have physical action, movement, or a dramatic incident happening — not just characters present in a scene. Describe WHAT IS HAPPENING at each moment.
- **ZERO MUSIC-ONLY GAPS**: No segment can be a silent or music-only gap. Every segment must have spoken narration for its entire duration. Music is background only — never the sole content of a segment.
- Ensure the narration flows naturally between segments to create a single, cohesive story.
