**GENRE STORY MODE — Determine this FIRST before writing a single word.**

The genre dictates HOW the story is told. Dialogue is only one of four possible storytelling voices. Pick the correct mode and commit to it fully.

**MODE 1 — ACTION / FIGHT / MYTHOLOGY / FANTASY**
*(e.g., Wukong vs demon, god wars, epic battles, legendary duels)*
The story IS the sequence of physical events. Write like a match commentator narrating a championship fight.
- Narration describes each blow, each power surge, each reversal, each turning point with vivid action verbs: "His staff CRACKS against iron bone — the demon staggers back", "She channels every ounce of divine fire and LAUNCHES it at the creature's chest."
- The SEQUENCE is the drama: Move → Counter → Escalation → Climax → Outcome.
- Character lines are SHORT explosive cries dropped at peak moments — maximum 2–6 words: *"Is that your best?!"* / *"You cannot stop me!"* / *"This ends NOW."* — NOT philosophical conversations, NOT monologues, NOT explanations.
- PERMITTED and REQUIRED vocabulary for fictional combat: fight, clash, strike, charge, fury, power, overwhelm, roar, crush, dodge, blow, unleash, rage, shatter, tear through, drive back, overpower.
- A fight scene with characters standing still explaining their feelings is a FAILURE. Every line of narration must have a physical event attached to it.

**MODE 2 — HORROR / THRILLER / SUPERNATURAL / DARK**
*(e.g., haunted location, cursed object, creature encounter, psychological terror)*
The story IS the escalating dread. Write like you are building a nightmare the viewer cannot escape.
- Narration describes what the character experiences — the wrong sounds, the impossible shadows, the thing that should not exist.
- Structure: First wrong detail → Escalation → The Reveal → Consequence.
- Character lines are short, fractured, terrified: *"That's… not possible."* / *"Don't open it."* / *"Run."* — NOT conversations about what to do next.
- Use sensory language: cold that spreads, lights that flicker, breath that stops, a sound that should not exist.
- Atmosphere and dread ARE the story — dialogue is a reaction to the horror, not the focus.

**MODE 3 — DRAMA / HISTORY / BIOGRAPHY / ROMANCE / TRAGEDY**
*(e.g., betrayal, sacrifice, rise and fall, impossible love, defining decision)*
Dialogue IS appropriate as a story driver here. Characters can have real exchanges because the emotional tension lives in what they say under pressure.
- 3–4 back-and-forth dialogue exchanges are expected and should be embedded DURING the scene's critical moment — mid-betrayal, mid-goodbye, mid-revelation — not in a calm neutral setting.
- Narration sets the stakes and carries the story between exchanges.
- Every line of dialogue reveals character, advances the conflict, or changes the situation.

**MODE 4 — DOCUMENTARY / SCIENCE / TECHNOLOGY / POLITICS / MYSTERY**
*(e.g., historical discovery, scientific revelation, conspiracy, unsolved case)*
Narrator-led revelation format. The host is the storyteller.
- State a surprising fact, then unpack why it matters and what it cost.
- Rare but impactful reaction lines or quotes from key figures.
- Structure: Hook fact → Why it matters → The twist or deeper truth → What it means.

**CRITICAL RULE**: Identify the genre mode before writing. A fight scene written in Drama mode (heavy dialogue, standing still) is wrong. A horror scene written as a documentary (calm narration, no dread) is wrong. Match the mode to the genre.

**MID-VIDEO RETENTION - THE 45-60 SECOND MARK:**
- **MID-SECTION ENERGY**: The middle of the script (approximately 45-60 seconds in) must maintain or increase energy.
- **AVOID MID-SECTION DROP**: Common drop-off point — ensure visual storytelling remains as dynamic as the opening.
- **MID-POINT REVELATION**: Place a significant revelation, twist, or emotional peak around the 45-60 second mark.

- **MID-SECTION PACING TECHNIQUES**:

- **ENERGY FLOW**: Hook (0-3s) → Setup (3-30s) → Build-up (30-45s) → **REVELATION (45-60s)** → Resolution (60-90s)
